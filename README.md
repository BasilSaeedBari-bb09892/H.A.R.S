## The Problem

Karachi sits at an average relative humidity of 70–90% for large parts of the year. For anyone running a 3D printer in this environment, filament hydration is not a hypothetical failure mode — it is an inevitability. Polymers like PLA, PETG, and Nylon are hygroscopic: their molecular structure actively absorbs water from the ambient air. Once absorbed, this moisture vaporises explosively inside the extruder nozzle at printing temperatures, causing stringing, bubbling, poor layer adhesion, and structurally compromised parts.

**The solutions available to Pakistani hobbyists are both inadequate:**

| Solution | What It Does | Why It Fails |
|---|---|---|
| Commercial Active Dryers | Applies bulk heat to a sealed box | Heavily taxed on import; risks warping bottom spool layers; expensive (₨8,000–15,000+) |
| Passive DIY Dry Boxes | Seals filament with silica gel | Only prevents *future* absorption; cannot strip existing moisture in a reasonable timeframe; stagnant air means near-zero vapor pressure deficit |

H.A.R.S is built to fill this gap: a system that actively pulls water out of already-wet filament using a combination of forced convection, phase-change condensation, chemical adsorption, and PID-controlled thermal regulation — for a total BOM under ₨5,000.

## The Engineering Challenge

This project is a deliberate exercise in multidisciplinary engineering. It cannot be built by a purely mechanical approach or a purely software approach. It requires both disciplines to work in tight integration.

### Mechanical Engineering — Fluid Dynamics & Thermodynamics

Unlike a basic "hot box," H.A.R.S actively manipulates the **Vapor Pressure Deficit (VPD)** — the difference between how much moisture the filament holds and how much the surrounding air can absorb. Three physical mechanisms drive this:

**1. Forced Convection via Venturi Acceleration**  
Standard axial fans produce bulk airflow, but the filament surface develops a thin "boundary layer" of already-saturated air that insulates the plastic from the dry air beyond it. H.A.R.S uses a custom-designed 3D-printed Venturi nozzle: by narrowing the air channel before it contacts the filament spool, Bernoulli's Principle dictates that air velocity increases significantly while static pressure drops. This high-velocity air physically shears the boundary layer away, constantly exposing fresh filament surface to bone-dry air. This is the primary mechanism for rapid drying and does not require elevated temperature.

**2. Phase-Change Condensation (Cold Coil Stage)**  
The exhaust air leaving the drying chamber is warm and heavily laden with the moisture it has just stripped from the filament. Before this air returns to the silica bed, it passes over a copper cold coil. The coil chills the air below its dew point, forcing the bulk water vapor to condense into liquid droplets that drain out of the system entirely. This "pre-treatment" stage protects the silica gel bed from rapidly saturating, dramatically extending the operational cycle before maintenance is needed.

**3. Chemical Adsorption (Desiccant Battery)**  
The condensed, partially-dry air then passes through a bed of indicating silica gel. Silica gel is a highly porous form of silicon dioxide; its surface chemistry has a strong affinity for water molecules at low partial pressures. At this stage in the loop, the air has already had its bulk moisture removed by the cold coil, allowing the silica to efficiently strip the remaining trace humidity and return the air to near-0% relative humidity. A dual-chamber design allows one bed to operate while the other is regenerated by a small heating element.

**4. Passive Thermal Buoyancy (Solar Chimney)**  
A secondary black-painted vertical exhaust pipe creates a passive draft via the solar chimney effect. Solar radiation heats the air column inside the pipe, causing it to rise and creating a constant negative pressure at the system's outlet. This ensures the loop never stagnates even when the fans are offline, maintaining a minimum air exchange rate at zero energy cost.

### Computer Engineering — Embedded Control Systems

The mechanical loop alone is not sufficient. Without active control, the ceramic heating element could overshoot the thermal ceiling of the filament polymer, causing the spool to warp and jam irreversibly. The entire thermal regime is orchestrated by real-time embedded logic.

**PID Control Loop**  
The ESP32 runs a software PID (Proportional-Integral-Derivative) controller. It continuously samples resistance from the 104K NTC thermistor, applies a standard Steinhart-Hart conversion to derive the actual temperature in °C, and calculates the error against the 45°C setpoint. The PID algorithm computes a corrective output — a PWM duty cycle — sent to the gate of a logic-level MOSFET. The MOSFET is the power bridge between the 3.3V ESP32 logic domain and the 12V ceramic heater circuit.

The three PID terms serve distinct roles:
- **Proportional (P):** Responds instantly to present error. Too high, and the heater overshoots.
- **Integral (I):** Accumulates past error to eliminate steady-state offset (the small persistent gap between setpoint and actual temperature under load).
- **Derivative (D):** Predicts future error by measuring the rate of temperature change, damping overshoot before it occurs.

**Safety Logic**  
A 10kΩ pull-down resistor on the MOSFET gate guarantees the heater remains OFF during ESP32 boot before the firmware initialises. The firmware implements a hard cutoff at 60°C: if the thermistor reads above this threshold (indicating a control loop failure, thermistor disconnection, or blocked airflow), the MOSFET is killed immediately and the fans are driven to maximum speed to dissipate residual heat. Fan RPM monitoring (via tachometer signal lines) detects fan stall and triggers the same emergency shutdown.

## System Architecture — The Dry-Stream Loop

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                      DRYING CHAMBER                         │
│   ┌──────────┐    High-Velocity    ┌────────────────────┐   │
│   │ Venturi  │◄────────────────────│ Ceramic Heater     │   │
│   │ Nozzle   │   Bone-Dry 45°C Air │ (PID Controlled)   │   │
│   └────┬─────┘                     └────────────────────┘   │
│        │ Fast Air Shears Boundary Layer Off Filament Spool   │
│        ▼                                                     │
│   ┌──────────┐   Warm, Moist Air   ┌────────────────────┐   │
│   │ Filament │────────────────────►│ Cold Coil          │   │
│   │  Spool   │                     │ (Bulk Condensation) │   │
│   └──────────┘                     └────────┬───────────┘   │
│                                             │ Chilled, Low-  │
│                                             │ Humidity Air   │
│                                             ▼               │
│                                    ┌────────────────────┐   │
│                                    │ Silica Gel Bed     │   │
│                                    │ (Trace Adsorption) │   │
│                                    └────────┬───────────┘   │
│                                             │ ~0% RH Air    │
│                                             └───────────────┘
│                                               (Loop repeats) │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
                              │
                              ▼
                    ┌──────────────────┐
                    │  Solar Chimney   │  ← Passive exhaust draft
                    │  (Black Pipe)    │
                    └──────────────────┘
```

| Stage | Component | Principle | Result |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Acceleration** | Venturi Nozzle (3D Printed) | Bernoulli Effect | Boundary layer shear; rapid surface evaporation |
| **2. Bulk Stripping** | Copper Cold Coil | Dew Point Condensation | Heavy moisture liquefied and drained |
| **3. Trace Stripping** | Indicating Silica Gel Bed | Adsorption | Air returned to ~0% RH |
| **4. Thermal Regulation** | Ceramic Heater + PID | VPD Maximisation | Safe 45°C drying without polymer warp |
| **5. Passive Draft** | Solar Chimney | Thermal Buoyancy | Continuous air exchange at zero energy cost |

## Roadmap: The Path to Ultra-Low Cost

The ESP32 is the right tool for Prototype 1. It has a built-in serial debugger, plenty of GPIO, and is universally available in Pakistani markets. However, an ESP32 dev board is significant over-engineering for a PID loop reading one thermistor and toggling one MOSFET. The end goal of this project is to produce a controller that costs under ₨200.

| Phase | Controller | Rationale | Target BOM |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Phase 1 (Current)** | ESP32 Dev Board | Rapid prototyping; serial debug; easy PID tuning | ~₨5,000 total |
| **Phase 2** | ATmega328P / ATmega2560 (bare IC) | Removes WiFi/Bluetooth radio overhead; cheaper; lower power | ~₨3,500 total |
| **Phase 3** | ATtiny85 or 555-timer PWM | Minimal silicon; no dev board; PID reduced to bang-bang or analogue circuit | ~₨2,500 total |
| **Phase 4 (Goal)** | Discrete analogue control (op-amp PID) | Entirely passive control; zero firmware; no programmable IC at all | ~₨2,000 total |

Each phase will be validated against the same success metrics defined in Phase 1, so that performance degradation, if any, is fully quantified.

## Local Sourcing & Fabrication Philosophy

H.A.R.S is built on a "scavenger-first" sourcing philosophy. Every design decision is filtered through the question: *can a student in Karachi find this at Shershah, Saddar, or Daraz?*

- **Fans:** 16k RPM server fans are abundant in Shershah and local scrap markets at ₨200–400 per unit.
- **Enclosure:** Standard large plastic storage tubs (Kenwood, National, or similar). No custom injection moulding.
- **Structural Parts:** Venturi nozzle, spool tray, and silica bed housing are all 3D-printable. STL files are in `/hardware/stls/`.
- **Electronics:** All ICs and passives available at the local electronics market (Saddar electronics district). No LCSC or Digikey order required for Phase 1.
- **Cold Coil:** Copper tubing coiled by hand. Available at any plumbing supply shop.

If you have a 3D printer, a soldering iron, and ₨5,000, you can build this.

## Building It Yourself

> Full build instructions will be added during and after the Prototype 1 build phase (M3). Subscribe to this repository to be notified.

**Prerequisites:**
- A 3D printer capable of printing PETG (for heat-resistant parts)
- A soldering iron
- A multimeter
- Arduino IDE with ESP32 board support installed

**Quick Start (Firmware):**
```bash
# Clone the repository
git clone https://github.com/BasilSaeedBari-bb09892/H.A.R.S.git

# Open the firmware in Arduino IDE
# File → Open → firmware/esp32/hars_main/hars_main.ino

# Select Board: "ESP32 Dev Module"
# Upload
```

**Tuning the PID:**  
The default gains (Kp, Ki, Kd) are a starting point. Run the system with a thermocouple or independent hygrometer and observe the step response. Increase Kp until you see oscillation, then back off. See `/data/pid_tuning/` for logged tuning sessions.
